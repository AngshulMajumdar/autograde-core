Marxism or socialism do not logically require dictatorship in the abstract, but historically many regimes claiming those labels converged toward authoritarian rule. One reason is institutional concentration. If the state claims to act on behalf of a single historical truth or a single class interest, dissent is easier to frame as sabotage rather than disagreement. A second reason is economic centralization. The more resource allocation is centralized, the more political coercion is available to enforce the plan. Third, revolutionary vanguard models often weaken pluralism in the name of transition. That said, the conclusion should be contingent rather than absolute: socialism as a moral or economic ideal is not identical to one-party dictatorship, but in practice weak constitutional constraints, centralized party structures, and suppression of opposition have often produced dictatorial outcomes.
